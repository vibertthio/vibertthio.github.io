Taste is weird. People say, "I just liked it," or "I just knew it was good." Then they stop. But there's usually more behind it.

I want to get past the shrug. I want to understand what makes something feel right, how we pick up on it, and how we talk about it.

So I started paying more attention to how people talk about taste, especially when they say someone has good or bad taste. I look for patterns. I come up with a method to build better taste.

# Three questions to build taste

Try this with two things, two dishes, songs, outfits, buildings, anything. Ask myself:

1. **Can I tell the difference?**
2. **Can I say what's better, and why?**
3. **Can I help someone else see it too?**

Let's say I want better taste in sushi. It's simple, rice, fish, seasoning. So the details matter.

**Tell the difference**: Focus on the rice. What's the temperature? Texture? Sweetness? Salt? Acidity? How does it look and feel?

**Say what's better**: I think the best temperature is body temperature, or tongue temperature. It disappears into my mouth. No chill, no burn. It lets the fish and rice do their thing.

If that doesn't convince you, maybe the difference is too small or the reason isn't strong enough. That's okay. I try again. Iterate.

# Sometimes, good taste means finding hidden gems.

And people who are good at that usually do well with one or all three questions.

They **notice details and differences** most miss. Like how one light in living room makes every face look warm and relaxed, while another just feels sterile. Or how one essay's pacing builds steadily, while another feels like a list of facts. Or how one song's strange shift in rhythm catches my ear, while another just loops.

They **know why it's good**. The living room is where I relax, so the warm lighting is better. The essay's structure draws me in without forcing it. The song surprises me without losing its core. There's a contrast, and they can name why one works better.

They **can explain it**. They make me curious. They give me language to feel what they felt.

# More than just three questions

But **timing** matters too. Finding a hidden gem means catching it before the world does. It means being tuned into the edges, sensing what's about to matter, right before it becomes obvious.

Taste isn't just about noticing and explaining. It's also about timing. People with taste notice the small shifts. They see when something raw or fringe is onto something real. They sense when old fashions are coming back with new life.

The best taste isn't just correct. It's early, but not too early. Just early enough to see the turn before it happens.

And yes. There's more to taste than just **perception, evaluation, influence, and timing**. Sometimes, it's also a signal, a way to show status, tribe, or identity. I will leave these to another day.
