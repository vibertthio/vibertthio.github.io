# Why I care about taste

I already gave the definition of good taste in [Build Better Taste, Step 1](#/p/build-better-taste-step-1). Here I want to focus on why I care, and the patterns I recognize from observing people with good taste around me.

The ultimate goal of practicing taste is to enjoy life while it lasts. How do I enjoy the day in front of me? It sounds like a simple question, but it is profoundly reflective, challenging, and fun to answer.

I aspire to be a life enjoyer. Becoming an enjoyer is a lifelong journey. Practicing taste is a handy skill on this journey.

Good taste matters to me because it gives me more ways to enjoy the same life. When I can notice more details, understand more contexts, and play with more possibilities, ordinary experiences become less flat. Taste turns enjoyment from something I passively receive into something I can actively practice.

# The fear: does good taste make me harder to please?

I struggle with a question: should I practice tasting the finer distinctions of coffee? If I get better, does it mean that I cannot enjoy the bad coffee I enjoy now anymore?

This question matters because taste can easily become a form of rejection. It can become a filter that says: this is not good enough, not authentic enough, not refined enough. But I do not want taste to make my life smaller. I want it to make my life larger. In other words, I want to enjoy more.

# No. Enjoying bad coffee is a creative challenge

The question resolves naturally when I treat bad coffee as a challenge instead of a static attribute. Bad coffee can be good with the right seasoning. Bad coffee can be good when paired with the right food. Bad coffee can be good when served at the right time, at the right place, with the right people. **Treating it like a puzzle makes tasting an active and creative process.**

Below are three patterns I observe in people with good taste as they play with this puzzle: they perceive more, understand context better, and keep practicing.

# People with good taste perceive more (pattern #1)

People with good taste can identify more dimensions inside the same experience. They do not only ask whether something is good or bad. They ask what kind of good it is, what kind of bad it is, and under what conditions it might become better.

They can find distinctively interesting facts in ordinary things: ordinary places, stories, views, music, food, streets, objects, or daily scenes. This is why good taste widens enjoyment bandwidth.

The more dimensions I can perceive in an experience, and the more densely I can perceive each one, the more surfaces enjoyment has to attach to.

People with good taste also recognize which dimensions are easy to distinguish and which are harder to distinguish. In sushi, the creamy fat of fish is easier to notice than the balance of temperature, acidity, sweetness, and umami in the rice. In music, a catchy melody is easier to notice than the bass line that gives the song its bounce.

They do not stop at the loudest signal. They learn how to enjoy the quieter ones too.

They also understand different schools of aesthetics. They can evaluate something according to the aesthetic system it belongs to, rather than using only one universal standard. For example, a minimalist room can be beautiful because it is calm, restrained, and precise. A maximalist room can also be beautiful because it is layered, expressive, and alive.

By adopting different aesthetics, they can enjoy more dimensions, even conflicting ones.

# People with good taste understand context (pattern #2)

People with good taste expand the surface area of their enjoyment. They expose themselves to more kinds of things, put each thing into a more suitable context, and learn how to condition the experience so that its best qualities have a chance to show up.

People with good taste recognize accessibility as part of the experience. Price, scarcity, distance, seasonality, language barrier, and availability all matter. Something is not judged only by absolute quality, but also by how accessible it is in a given context.

People with good taste identify good things on each level of accessibility. They understand that money matters: it often lowers accessibility, but sometimes increases quality. They do not blindly bind taste to status. They do not automatically think expensive, rare, famous, or hard-to-get things are better.

The best experience is not always the objectively highest-quality object. Sometimes it is the best thing available at this level of accessibility.

# People with good taste keep practicing (pattern #3)

People with good taste constantly push the boundary of their experience. They practice finer distinctions, explore new dimensions, and learn new schools of aesthetics.

They build taste through experience, experimentation, reflection, and repeated feedback loops. The more they experience, the easier it becomes for them to see patterns, trends, and differences.

They can acknowledge the complexity of an experience, but still distill simple rules and principles from it. They do not reduce everything too early, but they also do not stay lost in complexity.

For example, after trying enough coffee, a simple rule might emerge: I usually enjoy bright acidity more in the morning, and heavier chocolate notes more after a meal. The rule is simple enough to guide action, but it is not pretending to explain all coffee. It is a handle, not a prison.

Reducing too early looks like deciding "I do not like jazz" after hearing one difficult record, or deciding "expensive sushi is always better" after one impressive meal. The experience gets compressed before it has been understood. A crude rule blocks future enjoyment.

Getting lost in complexity is the opposite failure. It is knowing too many variables to make a choice: origin, method, tradition, reputation, rarity, technique, price, review, context. Everything becomes explainable and variable which makes it harder to enjoy since you are constantly optimizing.

Good taste keeps moving between these two errors. It makes distinctions, then turns some of them into usable instincts.

# Taste as a way to enjoy more life, not being better than someone else

So, the people I admire seem to use taste differently: they notice more, contextualize better, and keep practicing.

Common sense usually links taste to people who only enjoy the best things. But I want to break that mindset. My goal for building taste is to become the kind of person I just described: someone who can find more kinds of enjoyment, and **actually** enjoy them.

Good taste liberates my choices, while bad taste limits my choices. Bad taste traps me inside crude signals: expensive means good, cheap means good, famous means good, unkonwn means good, rare means good, familiar means good, contradictory means good. Good taste gives me more ways to meet the world.

In this sense, I hate that taste is perceived as a luxury skill. It does not have to be about status, money, or superiority. It can be a daily skill for pleasure, attention, and enjoying my time in this short but fun life.

I intentionally exclude the motivation of influencing other people with my taste or aesthetics. I also excluded timing, being on the edge of what is new, and being cool. There are two reasons:

1. They are not the main drive for me.
2. They all involve other people's opinions.

Taste becomes messy when it involves other people. It becomes a status game instead of a way to enjoy my life, which is my main drive. Yes, feeling better than other people is an indirect way to feel joy, but it also opens up a can of worms and creates more trouble, which is the opposite of joy. Therefore, I will leave those aspects of taste to another day.
